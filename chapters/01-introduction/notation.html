Notation Rules and Tables

Consistent mathematical notation is essential for clarity in spacecraft dynamics and control. This section establishes comprehensive notation rules used throughout the manuscript.

Notation Rules

Vector Notation

Definition 1 (Vector Representation)  

  • Free 3D vectors are denoted with arrow symbols: \(\vec{v}\)
  • Coordinate vectors are written in bold: \(\mathbf{v}\)
  • Reference frame subscript may be appended: \(\mathbf{v}_i\) (vector in frame \(i\))

Example: \[ \vec{v} \quad \text{(geometric vector)} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \mathbf{v}_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(coordinates in frame } \mathcal{B}\text{)} \]

Column Vector Convention

ImportantDefault Orientation

Coordinate vectors are column vectors by default. The non-standard inline notation:

\[\mathbf{v} = [v_1, v_2, v_3]\]

represents a column 3D vector, equivalent to:

\[\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

Quaternion Notation

Quaternions can be represented in two equivalent forms:

Matrix form: \[ \mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} q_0 \\ \mathbf{q}_v \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} q_0 \\ q_1 \\ q_2 \\ q_3 \end{bmatrix} \tag{1}\]

Inline notation: \[ \mathbf{q} = [q_0, \mathbf{q}_v] = [q_0, q_1, q_2, q_3] \tag{2}\]

where: - \(q_0\): scalar part - \(\mathbf{q}_v = [q_1, q_2, q_3]^T\): vector part

Variable Processing Marks

Different processing stages of variables are distinguished by marks:

Detailed Definitions:

Definition 2 (Measured Variable) \[ \bar{y}(t) = y(t) + v(t) \] where \(v(t)\) is measurement noise.

Definition 3 (Estimated Variable) \[ \hat{x}(t) = \mathcal{E}\{x(t) \mid \bar{y}(0), \ldots, \bar{y}(t)\} \] Estimate based on measurements up to current time \(t\).

Definition 4 (Predicted Variable) \[ \hat{x}(k+1\|k) = \mathcal{E}\{x(k+1) \mid \bar{y}(0), \ldots, \bar{y}(k)\} \] Prediction at time \(k+1\) based on measurements up to previous time \(k\).

Definition 5 (Prediction Error) \[ \tilde{x}(k) = x(k) - \hat{x}(k\|k-1) \] or more generally: \[ \tilde{y}(k) = y(k) - y_{ref}(k) \]

Stochastic Process Notation

Definition 6 (Power Spectral Density (PSD)) For a stationary stochastic process \(d(t)\), the power spectral density is denoted:

\[ S_d^2(f) \geq 0 \quad [\text{units}^2/\text{Hz}] \]

The spectral density (SD) is the square root:

\[ S_d(f) = \sqrt{S_d^2(f)} \quad [\text{units}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}] \]

Relationship to Autocorrelation: \[ S_d^2(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} R_d(\tau) e^{-j2\pi f\tau} d\tau \]

where \(R_d(\tau) = \mathcal{E}\{d(t)d(t+\tau)\}\) is the autocorrelation function.

Additional Definitions

Definition 7 (Discrete White Noise) A sequence \(\{\mathbf{w}(k)\}\) is white noise if:

  1. \(\mathcal{E}\{\mathbf{w}(k)\} = \mathbf{0}\) (zero mean)
  2. \(\mathcal{E}\{\mathbf{w}(k)\mathbf{w}(j)^T\} = \mathbf{Q}\delta_{kj}\) (uncorrelated)

where \(\delta_{kj}\) is the Kronecker delta.

Definition 8 (Sensitivity Functions) For a feedback system with plant \(G\) and controller \(K\):

Sensitivity (error to disturbance): \[S = (I + GK)^{-1}\]

Complementary Sensitivity (output to reference): \[T = GK(I + GK)^{-1} = I - S\]

Identity relationship: \[S + T = I\]
